Introduction

In your previous chemistry courses, you were often told what a molecule was. In this course, you will learn how to prove it. This is a "detective" course where we use electromagnetic radiation to probe the architecture of carbon-based compounds. By the end of this term, you will be able to take a collection of abstract graphs and translate them into a three-dimensional molecular structure.

Assumed prior knowledge and skills

This course is a continuation of the sophomore organic chemistry course (CHEM 128 and CHEM 129 series at Fresno State). I am assuming that you come to this course already familiar with concepts and skills introduced in CHEM 128, and you are able to:

  • draw valid structures of organic molecules and understand the electronic information conveyed by the molecular drawings.
  • identify functional groups, condensed formulas, and standard abbreviations of fragment groups.
  • distinguish between localized and delocalized bonding systems. I can draw resonance forms to show electron delocalization.
  • use IUPAC nomenclature rules to convert structure to name and name to structure, including markers of absolute (R/S) and relative (cis/trans, E/Z, syn/anti) stereochemical information.

Course-level learning objectives

This course is a study of the structure determination of organic compounds. After successful completion of the course, students will be able to:

  • Determine the structure from a set of spectroscopic data, including NMR (1D and 2D), IR, UV-Vis, and mass spectrometry.
  • Gain appreciation for the process of determining the structure of an unknown organic compound using a variety of spectroscopic techniques.
  • Understand the utility of different methods, their scopes and limitations, and how to combine data from multiple techniques to arrive at a structural proposal.
  • Use leading software packages (MestReNova and ChemDraw) to process, analyze, and report spectroscopic data.

In addition to the content-driven goals stated above, we will focus on aspects that extend beyond this class. I want you to develop a deep understanding of the ideas outlined above so you can communicate highly technical information using conventions and standards in the field.

Course workflow

Note

Our class meetings are scheduled on Friday afternoons, when energy levels can be low, and attention is already looking forward to the weekend. To reduce the risk of falling asleep, there will be minimal lecturing.

This course is designed according to the flipped learning model, an instructional philosophy optimized for active learning. Direct instruction will be kept to the absolute minimum and delegated to pre-class assignments and activities (such as reading and watching videos). Class time will be allocated to collaborative work and practice. This video series highlights the basics of flipped learning.

Active participation in class discussions is essential for maximizing the benefits of this class. Expect to be called out to solve spectroscopy problems in front of the class. Don't be concerned if you feel weak in some areas. The goal is to identify areas that need strengthening to enhance your understanding and refine your thinking process. This means that it is of utter importance that you come to class prepared.

Important

The next section is about grading and is by far the longest part of this syllabus. My grading philosophy is that learning takes time and that grading work based on a single point of data, such as a single quiz or test, is inaccurate, invalid, and perhaps unethical. A truly valid measure of learning has to involve multiple attempts that allow you to learn from (and correct) your past mistakes and demonstrate not only your skill, but also your growth. The grading system may seem complicated and convoluted at first, especially if you have only experienced point-based grading in your academic career. I am always happy to clarify the details.

Assessments and grading

The grading system emphasizes growth, and your work will be required to meet certain levels of quality that demonstrate mastery of the material. You will engage with several kinds of activities in the course, both to facilitate learning of the material and to demonstrate how well you have learned it. The grading system may be unusual, but in my view, it has several advantages:

  • Many assignments can be revised or reattempted if you need to improve your work (see the next section below).
  • If you're off to a shaky start and your marks early in the semester are poor, you have time to practice and improve, and past results don't hurt your final grade. The final letter grade is like a score in a video game---it stays the same or goes up.
  • Points, statistics, and borderline unethical approaches such as 'grading on a curve' are not involved. Your grade is tracked with a checklist.

Class Primer

You will first encounter problems in the Class Primer assignment, putting time and effort into attempting to solve the problems. During the class meeting, you and your classmates will help us come to a common understanding of the solutions through in-class discussions.

Grading: completion by the deadline. Not revisable.

Spec ID Datasets

Every few weeks, you will be assigned a Spec ID Dataset, a collection of spectroscopy problems to solve and write up in the form of a report that documents the process of proposing the structure from the data. We will begin with specta packets of processed spectra. Still, as the semester progresses, you will receive unprocessed (raw) data, and your task will be to process, analyze, and report the structural assignment. The problems in each assigned dataset will be a combination of problems discussed in class and new spectra.

Grading regime: completion according to given specifications. Revisable.

S3 Project

The best part about spectroscopy is doing it. Therefore, each student will receive training on how to use the spectrometers in our department, including the high-field spectrometer located in the basement of Science 1. In the Sample→Spectra→Structure (S3) project, your task will be to, as the name suggests, determine the structure of the sample of an unknown (to you) compound.

  • Level 1: Prepare a sample and acquire a complete characterization dataset
  • Level 2: Process the dataset and produce spectra for analysis
  • Level 3: Analyzed the data and solve the structure

Grading regime: completion according to the specifications for each level

Special application dataset

Naturally, 30 hours is insufficient to delve in depth into any of the techniques, nor is it the course's goal. Nevertheless, students who aim for the highest grade will be required to complete a special applications project, which highlights a particular NMR experiment to answer a specific research question.

Grading regime: EMR rubric based on completion and correctness according to the given specifications.

A few words about due dates[4]

Here is how due dates work in the workplace: They exist, and they're important. However, there's a certain amount of flexibility with them. If you need a little longer on something, you contact the person who set the deadline and ask if you can have some more time. This is usually not a big deal, but if it happens frequently, people will start asking you if everything is all right.

That is also how due dates work in this class. If you need an extension on anything, please feel free to discuss it with me. It would be helpful if you could propose a new deadline; however, if you request numerous extensions, we'll need to discuss alternative ways to support your progress. Note that you may not get timely feedback on work for which the deadline was renegotiated.

If you have significant extenuating circumstances that cause you to miss multiple assignments, please come to office hours, and we will discuss accommodations. I'll be flexible, but it helps to know what you need so that I can find the best way to help.

Caution

Class Primer due dates are non-negotiable. Class Primers are due before class so that you're primed and ready to think about the problems, and so that I can review the work and pick up common errors. For that reason, Class Primer absolutely must be done before class.

Revisions and reattempts

You can revise and resubmit each Spec ID Dataset once. Your initial attempt will receive verbal or written feedback, and you can use this feedback to make corrections and/or improvements as needed. Solutions are submitted on Canvas in an assignment area; revisions are simply submitted to the same assignment area as a new version.

Spec ID Datasets will come with a deadline for initial submissions. You must submit a complete, good-faith effort on the assignment before the deadline to be eligible to revise or resubmit it. Examples of situations where revision would not be allowed include the following:

  • The assignment is not submitted at all before the deadline (In other words, you cannot revise an assignment you did not turn in).
  • The assignment is submitted, but is incomplete. Parts of the problem are skipped or omitted, either partially or entirely, or include responses stating something like "I didn't know how to do this part," but without a good faith attempt at producing a solution.
  • The assignment is submitted, but the work has significant issues or errors throughout. This situation arises when it is unclear whether there is a grasp of the concepts, and will likely result in an invitation for a 1-on-1 conversation about the standing in the course.

Revisions of Datasets also have a deadline, usually 1 week after they are returned with feedback. No revisions are accepted past that deadline.

Academic Integrity and Collaboration[5]

Your primary goal in this course is to develop a deep understanding and expertise in organic spectroscopy. Collaboration and cooperation are invaluable in the learning process, and we will have many opportunities for collaborative work. However, because most work is revisable until specifications are met, assigned work must be done independently. For the same reason, copying or sharing of work should be viewed as unnecessary and counterproductive to your learning.

This course is subject to the policies of Fresno State and the student code. This document establishes guidelines and expands and clarifies these policies with respect to all work done in CHEM 140T this semester. Be sure to read it carefully and honor it fully.

The student code defines academic misconduct as any action or behavior that misrepresents one's contributions to or the results of any scholarly product submitted for credit, evaluation, or dissemination. This includes cheating, collusion, dual submission, falsification, and plagiarism.

The following guidelines are designed to help you avoid academic misconduct in your assignments. The list is intended to be helpful but not exhaustive. If you are unsure about the appropriateness of some form of assistance for an assignment, please ask me.

On assignments graded for correctness, every step of every solution must be one that you understand yourself and that you have generated by thinking about the problem. You are permitted to collaborate on general ideas and hints with classmates, but you must always write solutions independently. Collaboration on problems should occur when your collaborators are at essentially the same stage of the problem solution as you. For example, if you have not yet started problem #4 and you ask a friend (who has already completed it), "How did you do problem 4?", this counts as academic misconduct. The resulting work is not and cannot be considered your own.

Some problems will not allow collaboration. This helps to ensure that you can work independently to prove key results. In such cases, the only permitted help is consultation with Dr. Muchalski.

On all work, unless directly stated otherwise, the only resources you may use are our course textbook and your class notes (including recordings). You are not permitted to go looking for completed solutions to problems in other texts or resources. In particular, the use of Internet resources and other textbooks is entirely off-limits, including Class Primers. Often, complete solutions are posted online or in another book. If you see a solution, you can't claim to have an original solution.

Copying a solution, or any part of a solution, from any source (friend, Internet, book, etc.) in any setting, constitutes academic misconduct. On any assignment, basing your work on the efforts of another student who previously completed this course, or one like it, is considered academic misconduct. Use of any shared or solicited materials, including those posted on Chegg or similar sites, is academic misconduct. Class materials may not be shared with students outside of this class, including posting solutions on Chegg or elsewhere.

Consequences of academic dishonesty. Evidence of dishonest behavior on any assignment will result, at a minimum, in a mark of 'incomplete' and not being able to revise and resubmit the work. In severe cases, the penalty will be failure of the course. Peers who willingly assist others in acts of academic misconduct are equally culpable and will face similar consequences. In all cases, violations will be considered according to APM 235 http://www.fresnostate.edu/aps/documents/apm/235.pdf.

Remember that I want you to be successful. That is, I want you to develop a deep, personal understanding of the material we study so that you become a better student of chemistry who can go on to do well in all of your future endeavors. Every part of this course structure – including both collaborative work and restrictions on collaboration – is intended to help you with this. You will often struggle, and that's intentional – struggling (and eventually succeeding) is essential to learning. Indeed, productively failing (and learning from it) is part of your final grade.

In all aspects of the course, please understand that I am generous with hints and am always willing to discuss problems with you. I will never give you a direct answer, but I will offer direction and guidance that will assist you in finding a solution on your own. This is by far the most satisfying way to solve a problem, and the difficulty is well worth it. Please make frequent use of my office hours, email, and your team. You are always welcome to discuss your questions or concerns with me at any time.

Communication and instructor availability

You can ask a question about anything at any time. You have both the right and the responsibility to ask questions about anything in the course you don't fully understand, whether math or some aspect of the syllabus or anything else. You can email me, schedule an office hour, make an appointment, or grab me before or after class.

However, please note that I may not always respond to messages immediately. In particular, I do not typically check email outside of my regular business hours, and I do not typically check email at all on weekends to prioritize time for family and other non-work pursuits. That said,

  • Messages sent on a weekday (Monday-Friday) before 3:00 pm will likely get a response the same day.
  • Messages sent after 3:00 pm Monday-Thursday will get a response the following day.
  • Messages sent after 3:00 pm on Fridays or on the weekend will get a response the following Monday.

Please plan for instructor availability. For example, if you have something to turn in on a Friday, and you send a question by email after 3:00 pm, my reply may not come in time to help you; plan for contingencies like this.

Dropping the course after the census date

A serious and compelling reason is defined as an unexpected condition that is not present before enrollment in the course that unexpectedly arises and interferes with a student's ability to attend class meetings and/or complete course requirements.
